\lesson{7}{Mar 18 2022 Fri (16:34:47)}{Identify correlation and causation}{Unit 1}
\label{les_7_unit_1:identify_correlation_and_causation}

\begin{definition}[Correlation]
  \label{def:correlation}

  A mutual relationship or connection between two or more things.

  Two quantities have a \textit{correlation} if they tend to vary together.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Causation]
  \label{def:causation}

  This indicates a relationship between two events where one event is affected
  by the other.
\end{definition}

Let's take a look at a couple of examples for each definition:

\begin{example}
  \label{exm:correlation_and_causation}

  Maria and Andy are high school students in Arizona. Andy always gets an A on
  his math test when it's sunny outside.

  You may think that Andy is acing his test because it's sunny when he takes the
  test, but that's not true. It just happens that it's sunny when there's a math
  test.

  So, the two events: The sunniness and the acing his test occurred together
  without one causing the other. In simpler terms, the two events are
  \textbf{Correlated}, but there's no \textbf{Causal} relationship between them.
\end{example}

\begin{note}
  \label{nte:correlation_and_causation_note}

  If you have a correlation (\ref{def:correlation}) you don't always have a
  causation (\ref{def:causation}).
\end{note}
