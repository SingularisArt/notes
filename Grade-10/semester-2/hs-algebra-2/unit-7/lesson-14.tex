\lesson{14}{Mar 18 2022 Fri (23:06:17)}{Probability of intersection or union: Word problems}{Unit 7}

If $30\%$ of people at a certain conference are doctors, $32\%$ are women, and
$11\%$ are female doctors, what is the probability that a person selected at
random at this conference is a doctor or women (or both)?

You can write it like this:

\begin{equation}
  \label{eqt:probaility_of_intersection_or_union}
  \begin{split}
    P(D | W) &= P(D) + P(W) - P(both) \\
             &= 0.39 + 0.32 - 0.11 \\
             &= 0.60
  \end{split}
.\end{equation}

The reason we subtract $P(both)$ is because when you add up all the people that
are doctors and all the people that are women, you've already covered the
\textbf{BOTH} situation. Because, in the doctors, you're counting all the males
and females and when you count, you're also counting the doctors and not
doctors. If we don't subtract $P(both)$, then you will be counting the $P(both)$
twice.
