\lesson{2}{Mar 18 2022 Fri (16:48:56)}{Solve system of equations}{Unit 2}
\label{les_2_unit_2:solve_system_of_equations}

Let's just start with a simple example.

\begin{example}
  \label{exm:simple_example_by_solving_a_system_of_equations}

  \begin{equation}
    \label{cse:simple_example_by_solving_a_system_of_equations}
    \begin{cases}
      2x + y = 5 \\
      3x - y = 5
    \end{cases}
  .\end{equation}

  This is a rather straight forward answer. You just add the two equations
  together, which gets you: $5x = 10$. Now, just simply divide both sides by $5$ 
  to get the $x$-value, which is $2$.

  Now, just plug the $x$-value into either of the two equations to get your
  $y$-value. I'm going to use the first equation:

  \begin{equation}
    \label{eqt:first_equation_to_get_the_y_value}
    \begin{split}
      2x + y &= 5 \\
      2(2) + y &= 5 \\
      4 + y &= 5 \\
      y &= 1 \\
    \end{split}
  .\end{equation}
\end{example}

Now, let's do a more complicated example:

\begin{example}
  \label{exm:more_complicated_example_of_solving_a_system_of_equations}

  \begin{equation}
    \label{cse:more_complicated_example_of_solving_a_system_of_equations_1}
    \begin{cases}
      5x + 4y = 22 \\
      7x + 6y = 32
    \end{cases}
  .\end{equation}

  We need to manipulate these equations such that when we add them, either the
  $x$ or $y$ variables will cancel.

  So, we need to find the LCM of $4$ and $6$, which is $12$.

  Now, we're going to multiply the first equation by $3$, so the $4y$ will turn
  into $12y$. Then, multiply the second equation by $-2$ to bring $6y$ to $-12y$ 
  so the $y$-values will cancel.

  So, here's our result:

  \begin{equation}
    \label{cse:more_complicated_example_of_solving_a_system_of_equations_2}
    \begin{cases}
      15x + 12y = 66 \\
      -14x - 12y = -64
    \end{cases}
  .\end{equation}

  Let's go ahead and add the two equations, which leaves us with: $x = 2$.
  
  Now, plugin the $x$-value into any of the two original equations
  (\ref{cse:more_complicated_example_of_solving_a_system_of_equations_1}).
  I'm going to use the first one.

  \begin{equation}
    \label{eqt:more_complicated_example_of_solving_a_system_of_equations}
    \begin{split}
      5x + 4y &= 22 \\
      5(2) + 4y &= 22 \\
      10 + 4y &= 22 \\
      4y &= 12 \\
      y &= 3 \\
    \end{split}
  .\end{equation}
\end{example}
