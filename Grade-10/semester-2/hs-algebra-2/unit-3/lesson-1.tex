\begin{property}[Divide Powers]
  \label{prop:divide_powers}

  To divide a power by another power, subtract exponents.
\end{property}

\begin{proof}
  \label{prf:divide_powers}

  If you have $\frac{n^{5}}{n^{2}}$, you can expand each of the variables,
  which make: $\frac{n \times n \times n \times n \times n}{n \times n}$.
  From that, we can cancel the two $n$ on the bottom, and two $n$ from the
  top, which leaves us with $n^{3}$, which is the same thing as $n^{5-2} = n^{3}$
\end{proof}

\begin{property}[Raise Powers]
  \label{prop:raise_powers}

  To raise a power to another power, multiply exponents
\end{property}

\begin{proof}
  \label{prf:divide_powers}

  If you have $(n^{3})^{2}$, you can expand the inside of the parenthesis:
  $(n \times n \times n)^{2}$.

  Then, you can expand the outside as well:
  $(n \times n \times n) \times (n \times n \times n)$

  We can see we have $6$ $n$'s, which is the same thing as $n^{6}$, which is
  equivalent to $n^{3 \times 2} = n^{6}$.
\end{proof}

Let's simplify the following item. It has all of the rules we will go over:

\begin{equation}
  \label{spt:all_rules_in_exponents}
  \begin{split}
    \left(\frac{3m^{4}n^{7}}{6n^{5}}\right)^{4} &=
    \left(\frac{m^{4}n^{7}}{2n^{5}}\right)^{4} \\
    &= \left(\frac{m^{4}n^{2}}{2}\right)^{4} \\
    &= \frac{m^{16}n^{8}}{16} \\
  \end{split}
.\end{equation}
