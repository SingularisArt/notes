\subsubsection*{Imaginary Numbers}

What pairs of identical factors will produce $-1$ when multiplied? \\ $1 \times
1$ doesn't work since it equals positive $1$. The same is true of $-1 \times 1$
which also equals positive $1$. \\ $1 \times -1$ equals $-1$, but these are not
identical factors.

\begin{definition}[$i$]
    To solve this problem, the concept of the imaginary number \textit{i} was
    invented. The imaginary number was defined to be:
    
    \[ i = \sqrt{-1} \]
    
    With this definition, the square root of negative radicands, in addition to
    positive radicands, may be simplified.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[$i$]
    Simplify: $\sqrt{-8}$
    
    \begin{align*}
        \sqrt{-1} \times \sqrt{8} \\
        i \times \sqrt{8} \\
        i \sqrt{8} \\
        i \sqrt{4} \times \sqrt{2} \\
        2i \sqrt{2}
    \end{align*}
\end{example}

\subsubsection*{Squaring Imaginary Numbers}

You know that $i = \sqrt{-1}$ \\
Consequently, $i^2 = (\sqrt{-1})^2$ \\
Since squaring a square root will eliminate the square root sign:

\begin{align*}
    \sqrt{3^2} = \sqrt{3} \times \sqrt{3} \\
    = \sqrt{9} \\
    = 3
\end{align*}

Then:

\begin{align*}
    i^2 = (\sqrt{-1})^2 \\
    i^2 = -1
\end{align*}

\subsubsection*{Cubing Imaginary Numbers}

What about cubing the imaginary number:

\begin{align*}
    i^3 = i \times i \times i \\
    i^3 = (i \times i) \times i \\
    i^3 = i^2 \times i \\
    i^3 = -1 \times i \\
    i^3 = -i
\end{align*}

\subsubsection*{Biquadrate Imaginary Numbers}

\textbf{i} to the fourth power can be found in a similar way:

\begin{align*}
    i^4 = i \times i \times i \times i \\
    i^4 = (i \times i) \times (i \times i) \\
    i^4 = -1 \times -1 \\
    i = 1
\end{align*}

\subsubsection*{Simplifying $i$}

\begin{example}[Simplifying $i$]
    Simplify: $i^{27}$
    
    \begin{align*}
        27 \div 4 = 6 R \longrightarrow 3 \\
        i^{R \longrightarrow 3} = -i
    \end{align*}
\end{example}
