\subsubsection*{Difference of Squares Binomials}

One of the special products in this lesson is the difference of squares
binomial. Before discovering how to identify and factor these special products,
it is important to review how to multiply binomials using the Distributive
Property.

\begin{question}
    What do you notice about the first terms in each of the prdocuts of
    $g^2 - 16, g^2 - 81, \textrm{and} 25g^2 - 64$?
\end{question}

\begin{answer}
    The terms $g^2$ and $25g^2$ are perfect squares. Remeber, prefect squares are
    numbers whose square root is a whole number, or variables with an even exponent.
\end{answer}

\begin{question}
    What do you notice about the last terms in each of the products
    $g^2 - 16, g^2 - 81, \textrm{and} 225g^2 - 64$?
\end{question}

\begin{answer}
    They, too, are all perfect squares.
\end{answer}

\begin{question}
    What sign separates the first and last terms in each of the products
    $g^2 - 16, g^2 - 81, \textrm{and} 225g^2 - 64$?
\end{question}

\begin{answer}
    They are all subtraction signs.
\end{answer}

\begin{question}
    Now try working backwards. Find the two sets of binomials that multiply
    together to produce $g^2 - 49$ . What must you do to each of the terms in $g^2 - 49$
    to come up with the factors?
\end{question}

\begin{answer}
    The two binomial factors of $g^2 - 49$ are $g + 7$ and $g - 7$. Take the
    square root of $g^2$ to come up with $g$ and $7$.
\end{answer}

\begin{question}
    Why do you think the signs must be different in each of the factors?
\end{question}

\begin{answer}
    The signs are different so that, when the two sets of parentheses are
    multiplied, the last term will be negative and the middle terms will cancel.
\end{answer}

\begin{question}
    Will $25g^2 + 64$ factor into two sets of parentheses like the examples above?
\end{question}

\begin{answer}
    No, to fit the pattern, there must be a subtraction sign between the two
    terms. $25g + 64$ is considered prime because it cannot be factored any
    further.
\end{answer}

\begin{example}
    \begin{align}
        (g + 6)(g + 6) &= g^2 + 6g + 6g + 36 \\
                       &= g^2 + 2(6g) + 36 \\
                       &= g^2 + 12g + 36
    \end{align}
\end{example}

When you see a trinomial where the first and last terms are perfect squares and
the middle term is twice the product of the square roots of those terms, you
have a perfect square trinomial that can be factored using the following pattern:

\begin{align}
    a^2 + 2ab + b^2 = (a + b)2
\end{align}

\begin{center}
    OR
\end{center}

\begin{align}
    a^2 - 2ab + b^2 = (a - b)2
\end{align}
