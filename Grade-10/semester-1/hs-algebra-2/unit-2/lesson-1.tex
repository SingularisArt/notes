\subsubsection*{Greatest Common Factor}

A greatest common factor of two or more terms is the largest factor that all terms have in common. The greatest common factor of a polynomial should be factored out first before any further factoring is completed.

\begin{example}[GCF]
    \begin{align}
        3r^6 + 27r^4 + 15r^2 &= 3r^2(r^4 + 9r^2 + 5) \\
    \end{align}
    
    When you multiply ($\times$) variables, add the exponents:
    
    \begin{align}
         r^2 \times r^4 &= rr \times rrrr \\
                        &= r^6
    \end{align}
    
    When factoring a \bf{GCF}, subtract the exponents:
    
    \begin{align}
        \rm{To factor $r^2$ from $r^6$: $r^{6 - 2} = r^4$} \\
        rrrrrr = (rr)(rrrr) = r^2(r^4)
    \end{align}
\end{example}

\subsubsection*{Difference of Squares Binomials}

A difference of \bf{squares binomial} includes a \bf{perfect square} term subtracted by another \bf{perfect square term}.

\begin{example}[Difference of Squares Binomials]
    \begin{align}
        a^2 - b^2 = (a + b)(a - b) \\
        r^2 - 4 = (r + 2)(r - 2)
    \end{align}
\end{example}

\subsubsection*{Perfect Square Trinomials}

A \bf{perfect square trinomial} is a polynomial of \bf{three terms} where the \it{first} and \it{last} terms are \bf{perfect squares} and the \bf{middle term} is \bf{twice the product of the square roots} of those terms.

\begin{example}[Perfect Square Trinomials]
    \begin{center}
        Pattern:
    \end{center}
    
    \begin{align}
        a^2 + 2ab + b^2 = (a + b)^2 \rm{ OR } a^2 - 2ab + b^2 = (a - b)^2
    \end{align}
    
    \begin{center}
        Example:
    \end{center}
    
    \begin{align}
        r^2 + 12r + 36 = (r + 6)^2 \rm{ OR } r^2 - 12r + 36 = (r - 6)^2
    \end{align}
\end{example}
